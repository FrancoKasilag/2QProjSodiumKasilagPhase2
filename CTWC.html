What is NES Tetris and the CTWC?

Classic Tetris Mechanics

Before all the modern Tetris games (Tetr.io, Puyo Puyo Tetris, Tetris Friends, etc.) came about, there was NES Tetris from 1984, which is the original version of tetris most adults remember and a version which could only be played authentically on Nintendo's old NES console. This version of Tetris, was completely different from the modern games, as everything was made much more difficult and much more strict. You had no "hold" option to save a piece you don't want to use, you could only see one piece ahead, and pieces were given completely randomly (contrary to modern Tetris games that give you pieces on a 7-bag system, where each of the pieces appears once every 7 pieces). In addition to this, the blocks felt much more "sticky", meaning that once a piece was put down, it stuck to the board like glue and you couldn't move it anywhere, which made fancy modern Tetris moves like T-spins extremely difficult, and misdrops fatal if not extremely punishing. In a sense, you could say this was Tetris in its "purest form". The best players weren't distinguished by strategy, they were distinguished by raw physical ability to spam the NES controller's buttons, and their stacking cleanliness to adapt to the complete randomness of the pieces.

What's the CTWC?

Even with the rise of more popular modern Tetris engines, the people who loved the classic 1984
NES Tetris stuck around, and they created a tournament called the Classic Tetris World Championship
back in 2010. It was a single elimination bracket, and players played on CRT monitors with old school
NES controllers. The game rewarded a vastly different skillset from modern games, where flashiness,
absurd speed, and stacking creativity meant nothing. While top players of the CTWC look very slow
and boring, if you take time to study the specifics of the mechanics of NES Tetris, you'll find them
doing things like vibrating their hands on the controllers to move the pieces faster or manipulating
the input delay of the game engine using sometimes frame-perfect inputs (1/60th of a second).
